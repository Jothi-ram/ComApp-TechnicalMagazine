FEATURED INTERVIEW

Mr . Siddhartha S
Founder and CEO, Intain Fintech Pte Ltd.

Interview

Let’s wind the clock back by a decade. The world was facing a financial meltdown, led by the US housing crisis. The cause for which was, in simple terms, securitization of loans. It implies the conversion of loans such as credit cards, housing, etc into debt instruments. “To reduce this risk and increase efficiency, risk management and cost reduction need not be a trade-off anymore”, this became the idea that gave birth to Intain Fintech Pte Ltd and COMAPP brings you excerpts from an exclusive interview with Mr. Siddhartha, the founder and CEO, Intain Fintech Pte Ltd.

“Ideas are a dime a dozen, people who implement them are priceless”. What was the driving force behind your decision to start Intain?
Since the financial crisis in 2008, the compliance burden kept increasing and there existed cost pressures forcing the need to improve efficiency and we were entering a stage where improving compliance or reducing risk and improving efficacy always seemed like a tradeoff. I had a fantastic team on Artificial Intelligence working under me and we'd worked on some interesting projects with MIT on trade processing. I realized that it was time to try and see how we could combine the ideas behind Blockchain and AI to reduce risk and improve efficiency at the same time. Thus, with what technology had to offer us now, reducing risk and reducing cost need not be a tradeoff anymore. We picked one of the triggers of the financial crisis, which is Asset Securitisation, and decided to address the problem - one that had led to one of the economic meltdowns in the last 70-80 years.

You have worked in various aspects of Financial Services and are now working in AI and Blockchain technology. Having witnessed a lot of changes over the years, how would you describe the journey?
Most of my career has been in financial services operations, where in a way, I was on the other side of the fence as a consumer of technology, to use it to make our operations faster, cheaper etc., while reducing the inherent risks. Now that we are providers of that technology, where we are helping other financial services operations do the same, I believe we can empathize with our customers, understand their problems and build a product accordingly. The journey overall has been exciting because I have personally seen the way we have scaled and managed an operation with nearly 14000 people, trillions of dollars in Assets under Admin and Custody etc. and now we’re managing an organization with 30-40 people over the last 15 months, all of whom are AI engineers or Blockchain engineers with the desire and the ability to change the world. It is a big change, but exciting nevertheless.

In February, your startup had launched the product Emulya, with the objective of asset securitization. Can you tell us about it and its reception in the industry?
As I mentioned earlier, we were convinced that we could combine both AI and Blockchain in a manner that would reduce risk and be efficient at the same time. For obvious reasons, we picked one of the triggers of the financial crisis and decided to work on the problem. While it takes time to adapt to new technologies, the response we’ve had from the industry has surprised us, in terms of how positive it has been. In the last six weeks alone, we’ve signed on with two trustees, who will be using our platform, to make the whole interaction between the servicer, the trustee and the investor completely seamless. While the fear factor earlier would’ve been that the intermediaries would be hesitant to adopt technologies like Blockchain which are going to dis-intermediate, what we are observing is that these intermediaries, who by law and regulation have a role today, are adopting these technologies to improve efficiency.

Karnataka has invited experts via hackathons to integrate Blockchain technology into the e-governance system. Telangana State is actively promoting an ecosystem for Blockchain Technology. In comparison, how well-received is Blockchain technology in Tamilnadu?
I do not believe I’m well equipped to answer this question, as we do not work with government services, nor does our technology cater to governments as such. However, just as an observer, my viewpoint is that Blockchain is conceptually good for a lot of government use cases including land records and so forth. The issue however concerning Blockchain, is that it is ‘garbage in, garbage out’ and unless our land records are made clean and titles are undisputed, moving such data into a Blockchain might make matters worse. I am not an expert and hence I cannot compare Tamil Nadu and other states.

What was the biggest challenge you faced when you were starting Intain? How did you overcome it?
Overcoming challenges at an early stage in a journey sounds presumptuous. We are faced with challenges even today, some of which may seem insurmountable. Our biggest challenge, however, was probably the fact that we started, coupled with an NBFC in India and the plan was to make them a smart lender and, in many ways, create a conveyor belt using Blockchain to be able to finance that NBFC through asset securitization. The NBFC faced several challenges in its lending business, which would have made our financing proposition completely redundant. Thus, within four to five months of our formation, once we realized that the situation wasn’t going to be sustainable in the long term, we had to launch the product in the market and make it a third-party product that would be sold to other financial institutions. Within two months of this occurrence, we had to tap into the US market which is more than half the world's Securitisation market.So that is one tough challenge, early in our journey, that we managed to get through reasonably unscathed.

What are the essential skills required for a student to secure a position in your company?
To put it in a broad perspective, we are a two-skills company. We work on blockchain and hence we have Golang and Solidity developers. We also work on Artificial Intelligence and hence we have Python developers with strong math background. Speaking for ourselves, in the Blockchain team, instead of specific programming skills in Golang and Solidity, we look for top-notch programmers. Most of our programmers come with a background in either C or C++, with extremely strong fundamentals. So, if you have great programming skills, you would get into our Blockchain team and you will learn about Blockchain along with Golang, Solidity etc. In the Artificial Intelligence team, besides Python, we seek a strong fundamental background in Mathematics and Statistics, because we are not seeking developers, we are seeking R&D engineers, and hence we require a strong combination of mathematics and programming.

What advice would you give the students starting their careers in these changing times?
I won’t speak for the industry, but I do have strong views on this matter. Having seen and been a part of it for more than a decade and a half now, I believe what has happened because of the IT boom is, that most of the Computer Science programs, outside the top 15-20 institutions became vocational training courses to churn out JAVA and Dot.Net programmers and so on. So we started producing programmers, not Computer Science engineers, and that remains a challenge when we hire. For instance, for the Blockchain team we hired students with very poor fundamental programming skills, even though they may be decent programmers in specific languages. We need Golang developers and nobody is trained in the language. So, one needs strong fundamentals so that he/she can pick up any language, and such qualities are generally lacking. Secondly, with respect to our AI team, there are people with great skills in mathematics but poor programming skills or vice-versa. In contrast, when I was at NIT Warangal in mid-90s most of the Computer Science students were good at mathematics as well, given how the program in Computer Science was structured to have a large chunk of content in mathematics. Also, post-graduate courses in Computer Science comprise mainly of mathematics, something which I believe has been diluted in today’s curriculum. I believe that people today in their bachelor’s degree need to primarily focus on building their fundamentals in Computer Science. Understandably, job requirements can force a student to pick up a language which can fetch him/her a job easily, but focusing too much on that front can kill the versatility and their ability to adapt in the industry, which is critical, given the rate at which the industry is changing.

We, at team Comapp, would like to express our gratitude to Mr. Siddhartha for his valuable time and wish him all the success in his future endeavors.

5G and the Digital Divide

Pradeep Kumar S, MSc

5G and the Digital Divide


           When Reliance Jio made announcements about 5G coming to India a couple of months back, I was excited about what it would enable. Order of magnitude increase in bandwidth and more people on the communications channel meant that is not just humans who should be excited, but machines too. 5G enables a world where machines can talk to each other seamlessly. The 5G world is exciting but who gets to live in that world?

            The standardisation steps for 6G has already begun and we don't know what it will bring to the table but, surely, It will be something beyond wireless communication like distributed computing. India has one of the largest telecommunication infrastructures in the world. According to OpenSignal, India is way past the UK in 4G LTE availability but has terrible speeds (8.7 Mbps, South Korea - 65 Mbps). One can see 5G as a solution to bridging the speed and availability gap but it is not as easy as we think it is. What is 5G?
5G is the 5th generation cellular network technology. Every network technology will be allocated a range in the spectrum for communication. For 5G, the current allocations are low-band(600 - 900 MHz), mid-band(2.5 - 4.2 GHz) and millimetre wave(24 GHz, 28 GHz, 37 GHz, 39 GHz and 47 GHz). All the super fast communication will fall under the millimetre wave band(mmwave). 5G is not just about higher download speeds, It is also about the changes in the network infrastructure that makes it unique. The following are some of the innovations in 5G tech.
1. Small Cell - small cells have been around for a long time to boost signals indoors. Since millimeter waves have shorter wavelengths they are prone to scattering. So 5G deployment necessitates the use of small cell to compensate scattering
2. Massive MIMO (mMIMO) - It improves the capacity of the network, bringing more people online. It can also power a wide range of devices like IoT, Self-driving cars, AR/VR, mobile phones etc
3. Beam Forming - It is an intelligent way of concentrating a beam on the specific target. For example, in a moving car antennas can specifically target the car without targeting the surrounding making it more power-efficient and providing high speeds
4. Full Duplex - With 5G, Devices will be able to send and receive data at the same on the same frequency, which is not possible with the current 4G tech
The Digital Divide
A digital divide is any uneven distribution in the access to, use of, or impact of information and communication technologies(ICT) between any number of distinct groups. These groups may be defined based on social, geographical, or geopolitical criteria, or otherwise - Wikipedia
            Though 5G marks a major leap forward in wireless tech which could transform the world around us, it is also concerning that who gets to live in that world. The problem is in implementing 5G. 5G's fastest speeds are carried by the millimeter waves and they suffer from scattering by walls, rainfall and even humans as they are near the microwave spectrum. A decent rain will block your 5G connection. To work around this problem, companies propose to place multiple small cells around an area to boost the signal. The multiple antenna solutions make sense for a crowded city but in rural and suburban areas it is not worth the investments for the companies where there are not so many people to use the connection. But inevitably, the services for 2G and 3G will be slowly retired pushing people more towards 4G and 5G. There are a lot of other problems, infrastructure for 5G costs a lot of money which influences the tariffs. Devices are also going to need special 5G chipsets to enjoy 5G services. So is 5G technology for the affluent? 5G can make the digital divide more visible if not deployed evenly. The IMT 2020 standards which is the standard for 5G is due in 2020, so we don't know what 5G exactly is. Then what about the 5G plans available now, aren't they real 5G? They are trail implementations of 5G, companies need public support to test and find out implementation problems of these technologies and to know whether it is worth investing in the tech like the Marvel movies where the hype needs to be built before the shows land in theatres. 5G makes huge promises, but if that benefits only the privileged class, should we be excited or concerned? A Tew Thoughts on Technology

Rathin J , M.Sc

A few thoughts on Technology


          “So, Where do you think would be the greatest technological marvel in the next decade?”. I was posed with this question as a part of a group activity, where we had to identify a niche area where technology could play a major role in the near future. My peers were from different scientific fields, and I was certainly expecting a fiery discussion, where each promote their own discipline. I was going to do that too with computer science, but I hoped to provide strong backup for my case. I really wanted my answer to be unique. The jury comprised of experts in computer science and management studies, and impressing them was certainly not the last thing on my mind. But to do that, I had to settle on one among the plethora of options I had in my head and focus on justifying it.
          My immediate inclination was towards sustainability and how computing as a source of technological innovation could be used in designing a structured and phased approach to achieve that. The idea seemed good in my head. We have come a long way since the industrial revolution and each invention since then has had a great degree of co-existence with their contemporary human community. At what cost though? Emissions have taken the biosphere somewhere irreparable, and with the requirements of the society in terms of “emission-causing” devices and services at an all time high, it is impossible to cut them out too. We have been concerned about this, only since data on the criticality of the problem have been collected and made available. With the problem now phrased, if not detailed in my head, I began exploring potential avenues for fixes. One thing I realized is that there is no single “solution” to achieve total sustainability. Reducing emission ratio for power generation, developing bio-sustainable polymers and plastics, ecological delineation of biosphere hotspots, preserving geographic bodies and sustainable running of civic communities were the first sub-problems which I could think of. Each a separate sect of its own, requiring serious work and study at the intersection of natural sciences and engineering.
          This is a collaborative task, but I was dead set on identifying where I could make a positive contribution as a computing scientist, before taking the daunting step of posing the problem my peers. I figured I could prove a better case if I could see myself working on the problem. Obviously, I couldn’t come up with something clever enough to revolutionize the way sustainability has been perceived (in a few minutes, to be fair to myself), but everything ultimately fizzled down to two fundamental aspects of computing: data and optimization. With data available from the array of scientific experiments, “smart” devices, weather patterns and chemical readings, the next phase would be to design models which positively optimize the impact of at least a small section of the various sub-problems in sustainable growth. With time passing swiftly, I thought it would be better to discuss this with the group to get more streamlined ideas.
          when I was preparing to present my initial views having these as my mental note, I realised that I had misinterpreted the question. It was in fact “What would be the greatest technological innovation in the next decade”, rather than where. This made me cringe at myself. I had made the most fundamental error of not understanding what was required of me. In the meantime, there was an intense discussion within my group on choosing between cost-efficient space travel and wacky devices, built to be the Swiss army knife of televised entertainment. I couldn’t come across myself to contribute meaningfully to this discussion, but I did think about this direction, and my thoughts led me to this new perspective.
          Technology is growing exponentially and this is inevitable. But, would a common user be able to perceive all its facets? I have had trouble in the past to understand and use several features of my mobile device. Despite studying computers, I have a hard time handling exceptions, error messages and troubleshooting issues. The rapid advancement in technology has a serious bottleneck with the limited comprehending capacity of users and this is a serious issue. The user dialogue is such an important component of the device, that its success and failure may entirely depend on it. With the recent advent of innovative means to realize an electronic system and huge emphasis on how a computing device perceives a user, this could be something to discuss with a panel of experts!
          Despite my earlier concern about my ability to contribute to the discussion, I pitched this with my group, but was immediately shot down. They had already decided to go with the ingenious system which agglomerates the now “disjointed” features of the entertainment industry under a single umbrella. To be honest, it was an interesting pitch, a commercial pitch. Just that I couldn’t relate how it would positively impact the society apart from, well, entertaining.
          With the session drawing to a close, I couldn’t help but notice an uncanny link between my thought bubbles. Sustainability strives to modify people’s lifestyle - individually and as a community. All the solutions of the computing problems aim to identify some mean, some measures which ultimately aid in our long-term progress. And as for the interaction between humans and systems, that facilitates how well we use the technological solution in hand. A dustbin is not smart, but it still conveys the information on how to use it to the best effect - which wastes to dispose and which to recycle. Although a crude example of the aforementioned interaction, this is still something not many have a hang on. I haven’t got the hang of it. Every electronic device has particular instructions on their safe disposal. Although this information is accessible, we don’t seek for it. This has direct implications on emission levels, and this leads back to the problem of sustainable living. Only a slim faction of people work on designing solutions for contemporary community-wide problems, but it is the people as a whole, who must work to understand the proper dimensions of the solution presented in the discourse and put it to good use.
          Ultimately, it is education. Education would be the broad spectrum to answer both versions of the question. I thought this to myself as I walked away.

AR - A Thin Veil

Subhasri Manigandan , MSC

AR - A Thin Veil


          Virtual Reality(VR) is a concept that is familiar all over the world. Many of us partake in fun activities which have different elements of VR in them. VR is considered to be an innovative and highly appreciated technological invention. It is a budding entertainment industry that caters to all ages.
          Without our realization, a veil stands between the real and the virtual world, one which needs to be analyzed and explored. We have always parted that veil and scoured either side of it but never the veil itself. On one hand, we have Virtual Reality with no semblance of anything practical or logical, it is simply made up of extreme scenarios. This is where augmented reality(AR) comes into play. Augmented reality is essentially a perfect balance of what is real and virtual. It enhances our perceptions of the real world and helps us focus on important things pertaining to the situation in hand.
          A doctor may prepare for an operation using VR to visualize an entire patient, operate on him/her multiple times, and identify the perfect strategy to employ during the actual operation. AR will be used during the actual operation-to see real time status of the patient’s various vitals, overlay reference videos with that of the actual patient, receive tips and guidelines from compiled data (this falls under AI), get an enhanced view of the site of operation in the person’s body, etc. Similarly, a pilot’s display of compiled data can be aligned or arrayed on the real life situation itself, in a view which resembles that of a binocular. As difficult it may be to wrap your head around it, it is crucial.
          While AR has been developing in many spheres, there are some fields that have been using it for some time now. Examples include architecture, navigation, 3D projections and camera filters. Camera filters use the concept of augmented reality, wherein our faces are shown differently but still the original identity of our faces remain. If a person looks at your photo, after some scrutiny, they will be able to identify that it’s you.
          There are two sides to every coin and the same applies here. A famous example of the dark side of AR is Pokémon Go, which is well-known for issues caused by its rules and nature of engaging the player. Modification of reality by AR may lead to ambiguities. A warped or distorted view of reality sometimes leads to repercussions on its users. Most AR modules use the likes and dislikes of the user to design the environment. This gives rise to the possibility of people becoming attached to that world and refusing to accept reality, as in the case of VR as well. Furthermore, games using AR generally collect data personal to the user, thus leading to privacy issues.
          As is the case with every aspect of innovation, Augmented Reality offers an enticing opportunity to perceive elements of reality from an entirely new perspective while also bringing with it its own set of drawbacks, thus giving us yet another futuristic goal to work on.

Autonomous Weapon Systems

Krishna N , MSc,

Assessing the development and ethics of Autonomous Weapon Systems


          Technological advancements today have attained a point where the deployment of Autonomous weapon systems (AWS) is — practically, if not ethically and legally — feasible within years. The stakes are high; these weapons can bring forth a new revolution in warfare, after gunpowder and nuclear weapons. While many requisite notions remain to be clarified when it comes to armed drones and targeted killings, advances in the development of autonomy in weapons systems are already sufficient grounds for thinking ahead. The notion of so-called Autonomous weapons systems making independent decisions on the use of weapons illustrates the requirement to assess the ethicality of even developing them. Military technologies have already incorporated certain elements of autonomy. For example, the United States of America’s (USA) Navy Phalanx system automatically defends naval carriers against incoming missiles, as do air-defense systems such as the Patriot Missile system and Israel’s ‘Iron Dome’. The United Kingdom’s (UK) ‘fire and forget’ Brimstone missiles are said to be able to find ‘targets’ in a predetermined region without further human involvement.

In simple terms, an Autonomous Weapon System may be one that can select and choose targets of its own accord, enabled by current technology concerning Artificial Intelligence and Robotics which can provide the necessary physical platform, perception control, tactical – decision making, navigation etc. For example, the technology already demonstrated for self-driving cars along with UK-based tech company Deep Mind’s DQN System could support urban search-and-destroy missions.

The topic of Autonomous Weapons Systems (AWS) does not have a long history within the international arena because AWS have only become a policy issue over the last few years as technology has evolved. Thus, because of the burgeoning nature of the topic, there are no binding agreements specifically targeting AWS. However, in recent years, treaty bodies such as the one that oversees the Convention on Certain Conventional Weapons (CCW), have held meetings of experts on AWS to begin discussions on pre-emptive moves to address AWS. Moreover, numerous civil society organizations have been working together and in conjunction with the United Nations (UN) to promote awareness of the potential impact of AWS and to take definitive action in prohibiting their manufacture and implementation.

For the most part, countries agree with the need for 'meaningful human control' over the targeting and engagement decisions made by robotic weapons, which unfortunately, stands undetermined. AWS could violate quintessential principles of human dignity by allowing non-living entities to choose whom to kill — for example, they might be tasked to eliminate anyone exhibiting 'threatening behavior'. The potential for said technologies to bleed over into peacetime policing functions is evident to human-rights organizations and drone manufacturers.

I believe that the international community must take into account the ethical dimension of the militarization of increasingly autonomous technologies. It appears clear to me that potential development and use of Autonomous weapons systems to select and attack targets without meaningful human control raises significant ethical concerns. In my view, the following must be seriously considered as we continue to march on towards total autonomy.

Firstly, while the call to outright banning of Autonomous weapons are valid and can be agreeable as it is seen today, it may be wiser to continue to examine the development and deployment of such systems under the strict supervision of the United Nations and the International Committee of the Red Cross. Secondly, working towards eliminating the threat of proliferation of such weapons systems is necessary, as the effect of these weapons in the wrong hands, can be devastating. Furthermore, stringent establishment of responsibility of War Crimes including Autonomous weapons in lieu with the recent discussions at the CCW is certainly a need of the hour. Lastly, underwater activities are more difficult to observe than those on the surface, on land or in the air, and therefore we must invite consideration of what levels and types of transparency would be necessary or desirable for increasingly autonomous weaponized technologies in the maritime environment.

On a concluding note, I’d like to say that the status quo of current human battlefield atrocities is unacceptable as we have seen over centuries and it is important that technology in its manifold forms must be used to ameliorate the plight of non-combatant people and precious assets.’’

Li - Fi Tech

Bala Rajendran Kumar A , MCA

Li - Fi Tech


LiFi- stands for Light Fidelity which transmits data through illumination of an LED bulb. LiFi was introduced by Hass, who is considered as "Father of LiFi". Imagine that you are driving in an unknown place using only the GPS. Every now and then you may have to look at your phone’s screen to see if you are following the right route. So you use a smart watch. That doesn’t really solve the problem, though. You still have to take your eyes off the road, and look at your wrist. But what if you could just see the way on the way? Enter one of the coolest, sci-fi like inventions - AR glasses. This enables the wearer to see the road and the map at the same time. Let’s look through, er, look at AR glasses in this piece.

          Let’s start with the very basics. So the AR glasses look like any ordinary pair of glasses. But, the lens is made of partially transparent display. It will not only allow the wearer to see the world, but also infuse some digital images onto the display. As we would have studied in our school physics classes, light has to rebound off some surface and enter our eyes for us to see the object. In the real world there are plenty of objects and we see them clearly. But, to infuse digital images, we need to add some other things. We require LEDs and OLEDs that can generate these images. All the light that comes is put together and filtered through a device called the “combiner”. Essentially, this device is what “combines”, as the name suggests, the light from the real world and the artificially created light.

OK, we have digital images, but the glasses are worn so close to the eyes, so how do we see these images clearly? It’s all done by the combiner. There have been many attempts to get a clear, comfortable view in these glasses since the late 1960s. But in the end it boils down to a combination of resolution, field of view, weight of the glasses and other factors such as the aesthetics. One would like the maximum field of view and a crisp resolution of the images. That is, we’re looking to look good while looking at stuff that look good. But this level of perfection has only been achieved by Tony Stark. All we have is a trade-off.

There are two main types of combiners that achieve this traded-off result. The first is the polarized beam combiner. It combines light from multiple sources based on polarization angles. This is cheaper and doesn’t give very high resolution or very high field of view. But, the glasses will be lighter and more comfortable. We can get around 23 degree FOV with 1280x720p resolution.

To get higher quality resolutions, heavier combiners are required. These are called the off-axis, semi-spherical combiners. We can get upto 90 degrees FOV and very high resolution. But this technology is very heavy and not comfortable to wear on a regular basis.

To overcome this trade-off, a new technology called waveguides has been developed. This, more or less, gets the best of both the old combiners. This technology is very sophisticated and also very hard to design. It’s like a medium weight glass with relatively higher resolutions. The main problem however, is the cost. The research going into this field is also worth Tony Stark’s fortune.

Now that you know about this a bit, keep your eyes open and keep looking forward to developments in this technology. There is a lot more to come here.

Renaissance in Computer Architecture

Pradeep Kumar S , MSc

Renaissance in Computer Architecture


          The defeat of Lee Sedol by AlphaGo not just marked a milestone in the history of AI but also in the history of Computer Architecture. AlphaGo didn't run on a typical system, it ran on specialised hardware called TPU (Tensor Processing Units). TPUs come under a class of hardware called an ASIC (Application Specific Integrated Circuit). They are designed specifically for Deep Learning applications and offer higher performance/watt against the next best hardware configuration(CPU/GPU).

Architecture in a general sense means the process of planning, designing and constructing buildings or any other structures. Computer Architecture (ISA) is not so different, it is the science and art of designing, selecting and interconnecting hardware components and designing the hardware/software interface to create a computing system that meets specific criteria. ISA is just a specification like x86. Microarchitecture is an implementation of ISA like Intel and AMD processors.

Computing system transformed the way we see the physical world right from the way we talk to our closed ones to the way we exercise our rights as a citizen. With Machine Learning, things are going to get better (hopefully). The current state of Computer Architecture is given below.

The Sorry State of Security
Commercial Architecture design has always been around performance and power but security was mostly left to the software designers. On January 2018, Everything changed with Spectre and Meltdown. Spectre and Meltdown are timing-based side-channel attacks that exploit speculative execution. Spectre was not just an attack on the microarchitecture but the very definition of instruction set architecture. Almost all the processors in the market to date are subject to these vulnerabilities or their variants.

Speculative Execution works by executing instructions that are far back in the instruction stream before it is needed. It does by analysing the previous branches and history of program execution. It was the way to get faster by keeping the ALUs busy and get more performance. The guesswork of speculative execution works and it has been working for almost a decade. The most important thing is speculative execution should not visible to the programmer but it is not anymore. Since Spectre takes advantage of speculative execution the obvious solution is to turn it off, nobody would want their passwords stolen. The downside of turning off speculative execution is performance. There have been patches to fix speculative execution but they do incur performance loss.

The story is not over yet; after Spectre and Meltdown lot of variants are being discovered at the rate of one per month. The solution is to rethink computer architecture, and hardware designer must come with new architecture with security in mind.

The Old is New Again

TPU is not a completely new architecture, it is based on a decades-old idea called systolic arrays. Systolic arrays were inspired by the heart. Blood from the heart travels through all the organs and comes back to the heart itself. Similarly, systolic arrays work by fetching data from memory and pump it through a fixed number of non-programmable processing elements and take the results back to memory. The change is not just by the chip design but by the demand at the top of the computing stack (Machine Learning). Systolic arrays are making a comeback because Machine Learning required it. Things are becoming more domain-specific, companies are designing hardware for particular applications.

Everybody like Python these days because it is a simple and productive programming language. It is easy to test ideas out without the tedious process of compiling and executing code. But Python does not have what the compiled languages have, "Performance". The above image exactly captures the situation. Though there have been efforts from Intel and others to improve the speed of python, the standard implementation doesn't come with those features. Imagine you pick up your phone with the intent of studying or even doing something productive: but as you turn on your internet connection, the notification bar would pretty much be flooded. Once we dive deep into those shallow waters, we come back after an eternity or two.

The question I am referring to is not “can we?”, but rather, “Will we?”. Firstly, I don’t entirely believe that all the learned or learning population can adapt to the complete replacement of teachers by technology. In the end, though technology in learning can be competent and functional, it never can or will replace the “human touch” or human intervention. If it does do that, it means our cultural norms and practices might have become drastically different and these views will no longer make sense, even to me.

Interview Chatbot

Abhishek Rajkumar , MSc

Interview Chatbot


          The time and effort involved in a conventional interview process is arduous. From short-listing candidates, scheduling the interview, fixing the venue based on mutual convenience and availability of both interviewer and interviewee to rolling out offers to those selected after duly verifying the records shared, digitally or manually, a recruiter’s task is often laborious. An online interview system beats this by miles. As a part of this, a recruiter can effortlessly schedule an interview by just logging into the system and sending out a trigger to the interviewer and interviewee about the entire interview process by mail or other medium. A reminder fifteen minutes before the scheduled time for the pair is set automatically. The details are shared in an encrypted form through all communication channels so that one need not log in to the system. There is no manual intervention required or requested.

Whilst, the online system has considerably reduced the effort required, an interview chatbot would ideal, eliminating bias. The chatbot cuts back the struggle further by scheduling the interview in the shortest possible time and also rates the performance of candidates. Many interview chatbots use standard technologies such as Google dialogue flow for creation and configuration, Node JS and MongoDB in the backend and persistent storage. Google Analytics is used for analyzing the user demographics, behavior and pattern. The sole task of the recruiter is to request the bot to schedule an interview between two parties. The bot typically has a speech recognition software that takes care of processing requests and responses. The bot does all the work from scheduling, acquiring and validating the information using web APIs. Then, an email with an activation link for the corresponding interview date and time is sent. The video recorded during the interview is used in the evaluation of the candidate’s confidence, expressions, rate of speech, and coding style. The bot judges the candidates on different metrics and records the final result for the recruiter. The recruiter or the manager can also verify the decision taken by the bot by examining the recorded video, audio, chat and feedback obtained at every level of the interview process. This helps in keeping the complete process unbiased and transparent.

The bot can upgrade with significant features in the future such as matching a job profile with multiple streams. Further, it can also ask basic technical or HR questions to filter the candidates, administer online programming test, and aid in completing the post-offer formalities.

The new realities in Learning, Hiring and Working

Karunya V , MSc

The new realities in Learning, Hiring and Working


          There have been great innovations that aim to transform the concept of education, hiring and work as a whole. We are witnessing a tectonic shift in the way the world population is learning their career skills, getting hired, and spending the rest of their lives contributing to society, after making a living for themselves. Of course it's more visible in technology industry right now, but it's only a matter of time before its impact is felt elsewhere, and eventually, everywhere.

Let's examine each of them in detail now.

Learning :
Learning was in master-apprentice model well into the industrial revolution in Europe, the legendary Gurukul system in India and similar systems in the rest of Asia. Universities with a huge number of students and standardised curriculums followed subsequently, with their own pros and cons. With the advent of the internet age, and the new vistas opened by it, we now have the best of both worlds with the innumerable MOOCs offering almost tailor made, need based courses, but at the scale matching and surpassing that of huge physical universities. It is now possible to equip oneself literally on any technical subject with the help of these MOOC tools, and the vibrant communities around them. And the icing on the cake is that most of them are Free! By the end of 2018, over 900 universities around the world have announced or launched 11.4k MOOCs with over 101 million learners. This has tremendous social impact too, as this is a huge democratic enabler, giving a level playing ground to all those who have an interest and internet, with their more privileged ivy-league counterparts.

Hiring:
It used to be reputation, referrals, word-of-mouth and employer-assessment in the early days well into the industrial revolution. Then came the days of reputation of the institutions one trained in and the grades, which were reasonable indicators and first level filters enabling mass employments. With the proliferation of technologies, emerging niche areas, and the opportunities offered by the on-line world, Employers no longer want to limit their choices. Tech Giants like Google and Apple no longer require a university degree for some of their top jobs. Google's former senior vice president of people operations, Laszlo Bock, said that, "When you look at people who don't go to school and make their way in the world, those are exceptional human beings; and we should do everything we can to find those people." The days of “you graduate to get yourself a job” are long gone.

In the internet age, it's again back to your reputation, and referrals when it comes to niche jobs. Your reputation and referrals are your online contributions and presence. Great technical blogs, Github repositories with cool projects, expert level answers in stack-exchange, enviable ranks in sites such as Hackerrank, TopCoder and CodeChef, make one standout from the novices and takes one to the top in the rankings when it comes to getting hired.

Truly, hiring has completed a full circle!
Working & Career:
Today's organisations are more dynamic than ever. Reliance Industries, the petroleum and oil major, is looking to compete with Amazon and Walmart. “Reliance is working on creating the world's largest online-to-offline New Commerce Platform," said Mukesh Ambani, Chairman and Managing Director, Reliance Industries. And the workforce is dynamic again.

The HR website peoplematters.in says,
“Size of the flexi staffing industry currently estimated at 1.7 million people and Rs 200 bn is expected to grow at a healthy - 20-25% CAGR over FY14-19. India can very well beat other countries with the sheer size of temporary staff employed in the organized sector within the next decade”. Upwork is a thriving business, enabling an ecosystem of a huge number of freelancing developers and technologists connecting them to customers in need, who don't have to hire them full time for meeting their needs.

This emerging ecosystem of internet enabled learning, hiring and working, is enabling the knowledge worker to be in demand as long as she ensures her skills are relevant. I conclude with a short note to the student community: 'Github is your resume'. So, if you don't have one, create one today! Work in interesting projects, develop them, nurture them, for they are going to show your progress over the years.
